### Synthesis



#### A) Physiological markers



Physiological signals are widely recognized as objective indicators for assessing an individual’s internal state, particularly in relation to mental workload, stress, and comfort. The most commonly used and reliable physiological markers include cardiac activity (HR, HRV, RR, LF/HF), ocular activity (pupil diameter, blinking), brain activity (EEG, fNIRS), electrodermal activity (GSR, EDA), respiration, hormonal markers (catecholamines, copeptin, prolactin, α-amylase, cortisol), skin temperature, hemodynamic parameters (BP, BVP, PPG), muscular activity (EMG), and speech \[(Diarra,2025), (Iqbal, 2021)].



These markers have been extensively investigated across multiple domains, including industrial operations, aviation, maritime environments, and surgery, with a continuously increasing number of publications, particularly in industrial contexts \[(Diarra,2025)]. However, their application in musical performance contexts remains comparatively less explored.



Existing studies in music performance consistently identify cardiac activity as the primary physiological marker, followed by electrodermal activity \[(Volta,2024)]. Cardiac activity is particularly relevant because it directly reflects the balance between sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous system activity. An increase in heart rate is commonly associated with sympathetic activation and stress response \[(vanFenema,2018), (Moreno-Gutiérrez,2023), (Nakra,2014), (Sebastiani,2022)]. Furthermore, advanced metrics such as heart rate variability (HRV) and multiscale entropy provide deeper insight into physiological complexity and autonomic regulation under stress conditions \[(Williamon,2013)].



Electrodermal activity also constitutes a reliable indicator of autonomic nervous system activation, as it directly reflects sweat gland activity regulated by sympathetic pathways \[(Di Stefano,2024), (Kim,2021)].



However, the relevance of physiological markers depends on the specific task and context. For example, motion signals have been used to assess bow movement fluidity in violinists \[(Di Stefano,2024)], while photoplethysmography (PPG) has been used for emotion prediction in musical contexts \[(Kim,2021)]. In the case of wind instruments such as the trombone, respiration and motion signals may provide additional relevant information, as sound production relies directly on airflow control and slide movement.



These observations support the relevance of multimodal physiological monitoring. Combining multiple physiological and contextual markers provides a more reliable assessment of stress than single-modality approaches \[(Iqbal,2021)].



---



#### B) Musical performance



Musical performance represents a complex activity involving cognitive, emotional, and physical demands. This context may induce music performance anxiety (MPA), characterized by specific physiological and psychological responses \[(Callac,2022), (Twitchell,2025)].



MPA may negatively affect performance and comfort, but moderate levels of physiological arousal may also enhance performance, in accordance with the Yerkes-Dodson law, which describes an inverted U-shaped relationship between arousal and performance \[(Hernando-Gallego,2015)]. Excessive stress has been associated with reduced physiological complexity, suggesting decreased adaptability of the autonomic nervous system under high stress conditions \[(Williamon,2013)].



Several studies have demonstrated that performing in front of an audience induces significant physiological responses, including increased heart rate and blood pressure \[4–6]. Correlations have also been observed between physiological responses and musical difficulty, further confirming the involvement of autonomic regulation during musical performance \[(Williamon,2013), (Di Stefano,2024)].



While the influence of audience presence and task difficulty has been extensively studied, the influence of acoustic conditions remains insufficiently investigated. Acoustic environments directly affect auditory feedback, which plays a critical role in motor control, timing, and expressive performance. Variations in acoustic feedback may therefore influence both physiological regulation and performance quality.



This effect may be particularly significant for wind instruments such as the trombone, which require continuous sensorimotor adaptation to maintain sound quality and pitch accuracy.



---



#### C) Multimodal fusion



No single physiological marker is sufficient to fully characterize an individual’s internal state. Physiological responses are influenced by multiple individual and contextual factors, making multimodal approaches necessary for reliable assessment \[(Diarra,2025), (Iqbal, 2021)].



Multimodal approaches combining physiological signals, motion data, and performance-related information provide more comprehensive and robust representations of internal states than single-modality approaches \[(Goshvarpour,2017), (Can,2019), (Arsalan,2021)]. In musical contexts, motion sensors such as accelerometers and gyroscopes have been used to quantify movement precision and fluidity, which are directly related to performance and physiological regulation \[(Di Stefano,2024)].



Integrating multiple modalities enables the estimation of latent performance states using data-driven or mathematical modeling approaches. Such methods may provide objective indicators of performance quality and physiological adaptation under varying environmental conditions.



These approaches may also enable further investigation of the relationship between physiological regulation and optimal performance conditions, as described by the Yerkes-Dodson law.



However, an important methodological challenge remains: physiological responses may reflect both stress and emotional expression related to musical interpretation. Distinguishing between these components requires the integration of additional contextual and subjective information.



---



#### D) Ground truth measures



Subjective questionnaires are widely used to assess perceived anxiety and emotional state in musicians. The Kenny Music Performance Anxiety Inventory (K-MPAI) is one of the most widely validated tools for measuring music performance anxiety \[(Kenny,2023)].



However, subjective and physiological measures are only partially correlated \[(McLeod,1986), (Ciuk,2015), (Dang,2020)]. Physiological signals reflect automatic autonomic responses, while subjective reports capture
cognitive and emotional interpretations of these responses.



Therefore, subjective questionnaires provide complementary information and are essential for interpreting physiological data.



Recent studies have also explored the use of musical structure and performance characteristics as reference indicators, correlating physiological responses with musical expression and emotional intent \[(Kim,2021), (Sebastiani,2022)]. This approach may contribute to identifying optimal performance states, including the flow state, characterized by high engagement, optimal control, and reduced performance anxiety.



The integration of physiological, psychological, and musical indicators may therefore provide a more complete representation of performance state.



---



#### E) Research positioning



Although physiological monitoring has demonstrated its relevance for assessing stress and performance in musicians, several important limitations remain.



First, most existing studies focus primarily on physiological signals without integrating motion, acoustic, and subjective modalities within a unified framework.



Second, multimodal approaches combining physiological, biomechanical, acoustic, and psychological data remain limited in musical performance research.



Third, the influence of acoustic conditions on physiological regulation and performance anxiety remains insufficiently investigated.



Finally, wind instrument players, and particularly brass musicians such as trombonists, remain underrepresented in the literature, despite their strong dependence on respiratory control and acoustic feedback.



To address these limitations, this project proposes a multimodal framework integrating some of this :



* physiological signals (ECG, GSR/EDA, respiration),
* motion data (EMG, accelerometers, gyroscopes, motion capture),
* sound measurements (microphone recordings),
* subjective questionnaires,
* musical indicators derived from the score.



The objective is to objectively characterize the musician’s physiological and performance state and to evaluate its evolution under varying acoustic conditions.



Furthermore, while room acoustics have been extensively studied in terms of objective characterization and acoustic quality measurement [21], their influence on the physiological state and performance anxiety of musicians remains insufficiently investigated. This highlights a gap between the well-established field of room acoustic evaluation and its application to the study of musician performance and comfort.



---



##### Research question



Based on this literature review, the following research question is formulated:



**How do room acoustic conditions influence the physiological state, motor behavior, psychological perception, and performance quality of a trombonist, and to what extent can multimodal measurements objectively characterize performance anxiety and comfort?**
